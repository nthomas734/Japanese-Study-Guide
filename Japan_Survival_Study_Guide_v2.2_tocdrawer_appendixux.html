Part 0 — How to Use Your Tools Together

This guide is designed to work alongside Flashcard App, AirLearn, and Pingo AI. Your objective is to learn Japanese in the most useful order for real travel conversations: short, correct phrases first; grammar and reading layered in parallel.

Your learning priorities (in order)
  • Essential survival phrases that make you polite and functional immediately.
  • High-frequency travel interactions: ordering food (priority), coffee, hotels, daily errands, transportation.
  • Basic grammar and sentence structure in parallel so you can combine what you know.
  • Scripts roadmap: Hiragana → Katakana → practical Kanji recognition for your use cases.

What each tool is for

  • Flashcard App: memorization and automatic recall. Target: you can say it without thinking.
  • AirLearn: daily spaced exposure and listening reps. Target: you recognize it instantly.
  • Pingo AI: roleplays for speaking practice. Target: you can produce the phrase under pressure.

Weekly cadence (simple, sustainable)

  • 5–6 days on, 1 day review.
  • Daily: 10–20 minutes flashcards + 5–10 minutes roleplay.
  • Each week: add a small amount of new material (10–20 phrases), then reuse it in roleplays.
  • Keep a running ‘Active Phrases’ list: the 30–60 phrases you are currently trying to make automatic.

Part 1 — Core Survival Layer (Politeness + Rescue Phrases)

This is the most important layer. These phrases prevent small problems from becoming friction, and they signal politeness even when your grammar is imperfect.

Why this layer matters

  • Politeness often matters more than grammatical accuracy.
  • A single correct phrase can smooth an entire interaction.
  • These phrases buy time when you’re confused and keep the tone positive.

  • ___ をください (requesting items).
  • ___ をお願いします (requesting actions/selections).

Offline homework (daily)

  • Say each core phrase out loud slowly, then at natural speed.
  • Practice: すみません (sumimasen; soo-mee-mah-sen) → request → ありがとうございます (arigatō gozaimasu; ah-ree-gah-toh goh-zah-ee-mas).
  • Pretend you are ordering water, declining a bag, or asking for help.

Focus on confidence and rhythm, not perfection.

Part 2 — Ordering Food (High-Impact Templates)

Restaurants are template-driven. You can succeed with a small toolkit: getting attention politely, ordering with a template, handling one follow-up question, and asking for the check.

Typical restaurant flow

  1. Get attention politely (すみません (sumimasen; soo-mee-mah-sen)).
  2. Order using a template (これをお願いします (kore o onegaishimasu; koh-reh oh oh-neh-guy-shee-mas) / ___をください (___ o kudasai; ___ oh koo-dah-sigh)).
  3. Handle simple follow-ups (set vs single, size, drink).
  4. Close politely (ありがとうございます (arigatō gozaimasu; ah-ree-gah-toh goh-zah-ee-mas)).

Part 3 — Ordering Coffee (Fast, Small Transactions)

Coffee ordering is fast-paced. Your goal is to be concise, confirm hot/iced and size, and not freeze when asked follow-ups.

Typical coffee flow

  1. Order the drink (short and clear).
  2. Answer: hot/iced, size, for here/to go.
  3. Pay and close politely (ありがとうございます (arigatō gozaimasu; ah-ree-gah-toh goh-zah-ee-mas)).

  • Say the same order three ways (slow / normal / slightly faster).

Part 4 — Hotels (Check-in → Room Issues → Checkout)

Hotel conversations are predictable and repeatable. You can pre-learn the sequence and feel confident: greeting → name/reservation → passport → key → breakfast/checkout time.

High-value hotel intentions

  • Check-in smoothly and confirm your reservation.
  • Ask about checkout time and breakfast.
  • Handle a room issue politely (AC, towel, key).

Part 5 — Transportation (Stations, Trains, and Getting Unstuck)

Even with maps, transportation is where real-time questions matter. The goal is to ask simple, correct questions about platforms, exits, and whether a train goes to your destination.

High-value transportation skills

  • Ask where something is (platform, exit, line).
  • Ask whether this train goes to X.
  • Confirm direction and stop count (if needed).

Konbini interactions are short and frequent. Mastering them gives you daily confidence: bags, payment, simple requests, and polite closings.

High-value konbini intentions

  • Pay smoothly (cash/card).
  • Make tiny requests (fork, heating, etc.) if relevant to your deck.

Part 8 — Scripts Roadmap (Hiragana → Katakana → Practical Kanji)

You are already learning hiragana. Keep the progression focused on your travel use cases: recognition first, then speed, then confidence.

Hiragana

  • Goal: instant recognition (no translating in your head).
  • Use your Flashcard App daily, plus short handwriting reps if helpful.

Katakana

  • Goal: read loanwords you will see constantly (menus, coffee, signs).
  • Treat katakana as ‘travel reading,’ not just an alphabet.

Practical Kanji

  • Goal: recognize common signage and menu words (not full literacy).
  • Start with the Kanji you encounter in your travel vocabulary and contexts.
